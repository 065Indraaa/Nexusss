About the Strait

Geographical Significance

The Strait of Malacca is an 805 km long narrow stretch between the Malay Peninsula and the Indonesian island of Sumatra.

Economic Importance

About 15.2 million barrels per day of crude oil and petroleum products passed through the strait in 2016, making it a vital shipping lane.

Historical Context

The strait has been a major trade route for centuries, connecting the Indian Ocean with the Pacific Ocean and South China Sea.

Global Strategic Importance

China's Dependency

80% of China's oil imports pass through the Strait of Malacca, making it a critical vulnerability for China.

Global Trade

The strait is a key transit point for global trade, with about 40% of world trade passing through it annually.

Chokepoint Vulnerability

As the world's second most critical chokepoint after Hormuz, any disruption here would have global economic consequences.

Security Measures

The US military and its partners have implemented various security measures to protect this vital shipping lane.

Naval Patrols

Regular patrols by US Navy and allied forces to deter piracy and ensure safe passage.

International Cooperation

Collaboration with regional partners like Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia to maintain security.

These measures ensure the continued flow of global trade through this critical waterway.
